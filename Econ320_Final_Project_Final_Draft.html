I) Introduction and Motivation: ¶

Pre-kindergarten programs provide children opportunities for social and cognitive enrichment at a crucial stage of their development. While there's been a broad expansion in both enrollment and funding for early childhood education (ECE) programs across the U.S, the programs that exist vary substantially in both quality and structure. The links between income and academic performance have been well established. Students from lower income families typically score lower on standardized tests and display higher rates of absences across nearly all levels of schooling (Morrissey et. Al 2014). Famines with higher incomes are able to afford private schooling, even as early as pre-k. And it’s currently accepted, and research supports, that these institutions do provide better instruction, with students from private schools begin to outperform their peers as early as grade 4 (Braun et. Al 2006). Whats less understood is what specific elements of ECE programs contribute to the most to academic readiness and future success. How does the program environment play a role? Does the amount of time spent in the program center make a difference? Does the level of enrichment at home play a more significant role?

Our research seeks to fill this gap by analyzing how quality and quantity of enrichment at early childhood education centers and at home, contribute to a child's academic success. Understanding and finding the best way to increase the benefits received from ECE programs is critical for improving the standards for instruction.

II) Data: Massachusetts Early Care and Education and School Readiness Study ¶

Our data comes from the Massachusetts Early Care and Education and School Readiness Study. Which was conducted between 2001 to 2008, and collected data from 372 children and their families as they progressed through various nonprofit and for-profit ECE programs. The first cohort of students (130) consisted primarily of low-income students in and around the Massachusetts area as was not studied longitudinally, instead data from these students was gathered from a single point in time, subject to when respondents completed their surveys. The second cohort of students (242) were recruited for participation from their previous participation in the Massachusetts Family Income, Infant Child Care, and Child Development Study. Data was collected at intervals of 12 months old, 24 months old, and finally just prior to enrollment in kindergarten. In addition to answering the same survey as the first cohort, this second cohort was also observed inside the classroom, where additional data on school readiness was observed and collected from teachers. Because of these differences in data collection, and for the purpose of gathering the largest dataset possible, our research will focus only on the data collected through the survey, from both cohorts of students.

Variables ¶

After cleaning the data frame by excluding study participants who either did not complete or finish their surveys, we are left with 264 observations.

Explained Variable: PALS_Score - Academic outcomes: Phonological Awareness and Literacy Screening (PALS) average score
Our dependent variable of interest is a child’s PALS_Score or Phonological Awareness and Literacy Screening (PALS) average score. The PALS test is a diagnostic tool used by educators to measure academic fundamentals like alphabet awareness, letter sound knowledge, spelling, word recognition in isolation, and simple oral passage reading. The primary purpose of the PALS test is to identify students display early signs of reading difficulties and who might need additional reading instruction beyond what is being provided through the standard curriculum.By analyzing PALS_Score in conjunction with the independent variables below, we will be able to asses the factors that affect school readiness as well as their magnitude.


Explanatory Variables:
INCOME_Level - Total family's income level (USD), as measured by the scale below.

(1: Less than 10,000, 2: 10,001-20,000, 3: 20,001-30,000, ..., 10: 90,001-100,000,
11: 100,001-150,000, 12: 150,001-200,000, 13: More than 200,000)

EDUCAT_PK - Mother's education level, as measured by the scale below.

(1: Some high school, 2: High school, 3: Some college,4: Associate degree, 5: Technical school,
6: Bachelor degree, 7: Graduate degree)

RDays_Weekly - Number of days in each week that parents read children's books to their child at home.
ToyScore_Variety - Score for the variety of toys in the child's home.
Hours_At_Center - Number of hours in each week that a child spends at the education center.
CenterScore_Literacy - Center quality score: Support for Early Literacy Assessment
CenterScore_Environment - Center quality score: Environment Rating on Early Childhood Development
CenterScore_Caregiver - Center quality score: Arnett Caregiver Interaction Scale average score

III) Methodology/Empirical Model ¶

Given the current literature surrounding the topic of early education programs, and the variables available to us in our data set we have developed the following regression equation:

Academic Score = b0 + b1*Income Level + b2*Mother's Education Level + b3*Number of Reading Days + b4*Score for Toys + b5*Hours at Center + b6*SELA Score + b7*ECERS Score + b8*Arnett Score


Our model takes into account a wide variety of data, from specific measures of ECE program quality to measures of family socioeconomic status like income and maternal education. While current research suggests that each of the variables listed below will each be positively correlated with a child’s PALS_Score, we are more interested in accessing the coefficients and their magnitude’s to better understand which of these variables has the largest affect.

Our hypotheses are as follows:

  1. As family's income level increases, PALS score of child increases
  2. As mother's education level increases, PALS score of child increases
  3. As number of reading days in each week increases, PALS score of child increases
  4. As score for number of toys increases, PALS score of child increases
  5. As the number of hours spent at center each week increases, PALS score of child increases
  6. As Center Literacy Score increases, PALS score of child increases
  7. As Center Environment Score increases, PALS score of child increases
  8. As Center Caregiver Score increases, PALS score of child increases
  9. As scores for center quality increase together, PALS score of child increases

To start to justify these hypotheses with the data, we present the following correlation table, We see that each of our independent variables are in fact correlated to a Childs PALS score. Some more than others, and their significance remains to be seen, but the tables below serves as a good starting point.

The Lagrange multiplier statistic for the test is 9.82 and the corresponding p-value is 0.1114. Because this p-value is not less than 0.05, we fail to reject the null hypothesis. We do not have sufficient evidence to say that heteroscedasticity is present in the regression model. This makes the T-stat and F-stat become more reliable.

At alpha = 0.05, we notice that only the income factor and intercept show consistently statistically significant results. We fail to reject the null that other variables are statistically insignificant, meaning that they might not have an effect on school performance among the studied subjects. We will then test the joint significance of the variables (excluding income) by taking an F-test.

IV) Conclusion ¶

In conclusion, we can not say that any of our variables, aside from income have any statistically significant affect on a child’s PALS score. This comes as a surprise, research suggested that our variables, which encompassed factors both inside and outside of the ECE program would each have a positive effect. And a while each of our regression coefficients was indeed positive, none aside from our intercept and coefficient for income where significant using an alpha of 0.05 in our final model.

Knowing that income does have an effect next raises the question of how. How and through what channels does economic capital have to flow through in order to create positive outcomes in early education? Or correlation matrix reveals that income is positively correlated with the ratings for ECE environment, as well as the scores for the variety of toys at home and the amount of reading time a child the receives. This intuitively makes sense, famines with higher incomes can afford to send their child to ’better’ private ECE programs, as well as provide more enrichment at home. But the results from our regression show us that both of these factors have little to no effect. More so our results from our second F-test show us that ECE programs that rank higher in quality are no more likely to improve a child’s PALS performance.

Overall our results reveal more questions than they answer, but it’s worth noting a few limitations to our design that might have impacted our results. The first has to do with sample size and selection. After data cleaning we were left with only 264 usable observations and ideally we would have many hundred, if not thousands more, that included data from outside of the Massachusetts area. We advocate for a larger study, with a broader scope of both participants and survey questions. Such a study could find that some of the variables we included here are indeed significant, and if it’s findings would be more generalizable to ECE programs across the country, not just those in and around the Massachusetts area.
